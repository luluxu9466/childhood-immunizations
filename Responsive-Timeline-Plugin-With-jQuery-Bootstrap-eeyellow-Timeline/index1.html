PERTUSSIS
1940
Pertussis Outbreaks


Before vaccines, an average of 178,171 cases was reported in the U.S. 93% of reported cases occurred in children under 10 years of age.

After vaccinations were introduced in the 1940s, pertussis incidence fell dramatically to approximately 1,000 by 1976.Incidence rates have increased since 1980.


In 2012, nearly 50,000 cases of pertussis were reported in the United States, the most cases since 1955. Twenty pertussis deaths were reported in 2012.
Fifteen of those deaths occurred in infants too young to have received the vaccine.


An estimated 16.3 million people worldwide were infected in 2015, pertussis resulted in 58,700 deaths – down from 138,000 deaths in 1990.




The average annual infant mortality rate attributed to pertussis was 3.8 per 1,000,000 live births, and 13.1 per 1,000,000 live births for infants aged <2 months.

MUMPS
1967
Mumps Outbreaks


Prior to the start of mumps vaccination in 1948, outbreaks during the winter/spring would commonly affect young school children with secondary spread to other family members not yet immune.

Until an effective vaccine program was introduced, isolation of the infected individual was the only public health control option.

Every few years, the number of reported mumps cases spikes due to an outbreak, usually isolated to a specific community or region of the country.


United States implemented its mumps vaccination program in 1967.

In 2006, a multi-state mumps outbreak, more than 6,500 reported cases: affected college-aged students in the Midwest.

From 2009 to 2010, two large outbreaks:
* Over 3,000 people, mostly students of a close-knit religious community in NYC.
* About 500 people, mostly school-aged children, in the U.S. Territory of Guam.


* U.S. mumps outbreaks started to increase in late 2015.January 2016 to June 2017, health departments reported 150 outbreaks (9,200 cases), schools, universities, athletics teams and facilities, church groups, workplaces, and large parties events.


* Largest outbreak ocurred in a community in northwest Arkansas nearly 3,000 cases.
* Two outbreaks from Iowa and Illinois each involved several hundred university students.

MEASELS
1968
Measels Outbreaks



Measles killed one fifth of Hawaii's and Fiji’s population within a decade.

In 1912 became notifiable disease in the U.S., requiring healthcare providers to report all diagnosed cases.

In the first decade of reporting, an average of 6,000 measles-related deaths were reported each year.

It is estimated 3 to 4 million people in the United States were infected each year, an estimated 400 to 500 people died.



In 1968, an improved and even weaker measles vaccine, developed by Maurice Hilleman and colleagues, began to be distributed.




A 1989 measles outbreaks among vaccinated school-aged children prompted the Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices to recommend a second dose of MMR vaccine for all children.

Prognosis:
Most people survive measles, though in some cases, complications may occur.
About 1 in 4 individuals will be hospitalized and 1–2 in 1000 will die.
Complications are more likely in children under age 5 and adults over age 20.


Measles was declared eliminated (absence of continuous disease transmission for greater than 12 months) from the United States in 2000.

Measles vaccination has prevented an estimated 20.3 million deaths.
